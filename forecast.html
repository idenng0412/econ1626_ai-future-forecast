AI & Futures of Global Logistics

Economic Transformation by 2030

by Thien Nhan Nguyen – S3972179

As agentic AI systems mature and adoption scales beyond pilots by 2030, AI will reshape global logistics by reducing uncertainty and lowering coordination costs across supply networks, although these benefits will be distributed unevenly.

Thesis Statement

Global logistics networks underpin modern trade, but persistent forecasting errors, inventory misallocation, and transport delays continue to impose significant costs on firms and consumers through higher operating expenses, disrupted production, and inflationary pressure.

This forecast argues that, as agentic systems mature and adoption scales beyond pilots by 2030, AI will reshape global logistics by reducing uncertainty and lowering coordination costs across supply networks, although these benefits will be distributed unevenly. Forecasting and optimisation can cut logistics costs by 5 to 20 per cent (Oca et al. 2024), yet these gains compound with data scale, favouring hyperscale platforms and large logistics providers over smaller suppliers. As a general-purpose technology, AI extends beyond physical automation into cognitive tasks such as demand forecasting, route optimisation, and operational management. In doing so, it shifts competitive advantage away from labour-cost arbitrage and towards data-driven coordination. While some efficiency gains may reach consumers through lower prices and improved services, the largest share is likely to be captured by data-rich firms, while routine logistics workers and developing economies face greater displacement risk. Consequently, AI's economic impact will depend on policy choices surrounding competition, workforce transition, and data governance.

Core Capabilities

Predictive Analytics and Firm-Level Concentration

AI enables predictive analytics by processing large volumes of real-time data into accurate forecasts, reducing the uncertainty that forces firms to hold costly safety stock and over-provision capacity. As forecasts tighten, firms run leaner inventories, suffer fewer stockouts, and increase vehicle and warehouse utilisation, which can lower logistics costs by 5 to 20 percent and inventory by 20 to 30 percent (Oca et al. 2024). Mechanically, these gains widen margins and pass partly to consumers as lower prices, while dampening the bullwhip effect through improved allocative efficiency (Lee, Padmanabhan & Whang 1997).

However, forecasting accuracy depends on data scale, where firms with the largest datasets can train the better models and win more business, creating a self-reinforcing flywheel (Jones & Tonetti 2020). As agentic systems become deeply involved in planning and execution across the chain by 2030, this process intensifies the divergence at the firm level, where value gains concentrate among hyperscalers and established firms while smaller ones struggle to keep pace (Singla et al. 2025).

Warehouse Automation, Labour Displacement and the Productivity-Wage Gap

AI-powered robotics and computer vision systems are increasingly automating handling activities, allowing warehouses to process higher volumes with fewer errors and fewer labour hours, reducing unit costs across the chain.

However, these gains accrue mainly to firms. For example, Amazon expects to automate up to 75 percent of its operations by 2033, while routine occupations in warehousing and logistics are left with increasing automation pressure (Weise and Kask 2025). Although automation creates new job opportunities, it displaces workers from existing tasks faster than new tasks are reinstated, while the reinstated positions are concentrated in maintenance and engineering, which demand higher technical expertise than the jobs being displaced (Acemoglu and Restrepo 2019). AI therefore complements higher-skilled workers while substituting for routine ones.

As machines handle routine tasks more efficiently and firms become less dependent on routine labour, productivity gains may fail to translate into higher wages and can weaken workers' bargaining power. Consequently, labour's share of income may decline despite rising output per worker, as firms retain a larger proportion of the gains as profit rather than distributing them through higher wages. As automation spreads towards 2030, this productivity–wage gap is likely to widen, with the benefits of automation accruing disproportionately to capital owners and higher-skilled workers.

Route Optimisation and Global Supply Chain Coordination

AI-driven coordination systems analyse traffic conditions, weather disruptions, supplier performance, and delivery schedules to improve decision-making across supply networks. This allows firms to manage increasingly complex supply chains more efficiently and reduce transaction and coordination costs while improving delivery reliability.

By making complex networks cheaper to coordinate, AI reshapes firms’ decisions about what to make in-house and where to locate production, which transaction-cost economics treats as central to supply-chain structure (Coase 1937; Williamson 1985). Although lower coordination costs alone could further support offshoring, automation changes the calculation by reducing firms’ dependence on production labour, weakening the cheap-labour advantage on which many developing-country supply chains were built (Baglioni 2021). As the cost savings from locating production in low-wage economies decline, firms become less dependent on geographically dispersed supply chains and more likely to favour regionalisation or reshoring.

Evidence of this shift is already visible: approximately two million manufacturing jobs have returned to the United States since 2010, with automation identified as a key driver (Moser and Kelley 2024). By 2030, increasingly autonomous supply chain coordination is likely to accelerate this trend, shifting competitive advantage away from low-cost labour and towards data, technology, and organisational capabilities. While advanced economies may benefit from increased domestic production, developing economies that rely on labour-cost advantages could face declining competitiveness and reduced participation in global supply chains.

Baseline: AI-Augmented Logistics

Under this scenario, AI-enabled predictive analytics, warehouse automation, and route optimisation become standard operational tools across the logistics sector, yet value remains concentrated among hyperscalers, while AI complements rather than replaces human decision-making. Diffusion lags as data readiness, integration, and skills shortages continue to constrain adoption, resulting in productivity gains emerging gradually rather than through sudden disruption. This scenario reflects current evidence that most firms use AI, yet few capture clear earnings gains (Singla et al. 2025).

At the Firm Level

Logistics costs continue to fall as forecasting accuracy and operational efficiency improve, but productivity gains remain concentrated among high performers whose superior access to data, computing resources, and capital is difficult for smaller ones to replicate. However, the concentration channel builds slowly as diffusion lag gives smaller firms room to adapt while data-rich incumbents pull ahead.

At the Labour Level

Displacement is partial and uneven, cushioned by persistent labour shortages that absorb some workers released by automation. As routine roles decline and technical roles grow, governments implement moderate retraining initiatives that help some displaced workers transition.

At the Global Level

Regionalisation proceeds steadily as coordination costs fall and automation reduces dependence on labour-cost arbitrage; the offshore base erodes gradually rather than collapsing, while policy remains reactive rather than anticipatory.

By 2030: Moderately more productive and more concentrated industry with inequalities between firms and workers widen gradually rather than dramatically, but the extremes of broad sharing and runaway concentration are both avoided.

Upside: Autonomous Supply Networks

Under this scenario, advances in agentic AI systems combine with wider access to data, cloud infrastructure, and affordable automation tools, lowering adoption barriers for small- and mid-sized firms. As technologies once reserved for hyperscalers become more widely available, the adoption and development of autonomous systems accelerate across supply networks. However, this outcome depends on active reskilling initiatives, competition rules, and data-access policies that shape how productivity gains are distributed.

At the Firm Level

Advances in agentic AI systems combine with wider access to data, cloud infrastructure, and affordable automation tools, lowering adoption barriers for small- and mid-sized firms. Competition and interoperability rules keep markets contestable, allowing smaller firms to compete rather than merely adapt.

At the Labour Level

Governments successfully manage labour-market transition through large-scale investment in reskilling programmes. New occupations emerge quickly enough to absorb displaced workers into higher-value roles, preventing large-scale unemployment and limiting the widening of the productivity–wage gap.

At the Global Level

Developing economies gain access to the same tools as advanced economies, allowing them to move up the value chain rather than simply losing their cost advantage. This creates a more productive and resilient logistics system while consumers benefit from lower prices, faster delivery and greater product availability.

By 2030: AI becomes a source of widespread prosperity rather than concentrated advantage, creating a more productive and resilient logistics system while consumers benefit from lower prices, faster delivery and greater product availability.

Downside: Concentrated and Fragile Logistics

Under the downside scenario, AI adoption succeeds technologically but produces significant economic and social challenges. Large logistics platforms achieve substantial productivity gains through automation and data-driven coordination, but weak competition policies and absent reskilling support allow these gains to concentrate among a few dominant firms.

At the Firm Level

Data scale becomes the critical source of competitive advantage, pushing the concentration channel to its extreme. In this case, incumbents capture most productivity gains and increase barriers to entry as smaller firms struggle to replicate the datasets, computing infrastructure, and capital investment required to compete. This results in a winner-take-most market rather than gradual concentration.

At the Labour Level

Displacement accelerates due to weak reabsorption as automation displaces routine workers faster than new roles emerge, while labour's wage share falls sharply and inequality widens due to the absence of regulatory protection and government support. It is projected that more than 92 million roles could be displaced by emerging technologies by 2030, highlighting the scale of disruption if transition policies fail to keep pace (World Economic Forum 2025).

At the Global Level

Developing economies that relied on labour-cost arbitrage experience declining participation in global supply chains, deepening economic divergence between advanced and developing economies.

By 2030: Technological success with more productive logistics networks but significant economic and social challenges with concentrated gains and widespread displacement that generate public resistance to further AI adoption, as broader social welfare benefits are left unchecked.

1. Competition and Data Access Policy

Because AI performance relies on data scale, large firms can leverage this advantage to capture markets and raise barriers to entry, a self-reinforcing dynamic that tips the market toward winner-take-most. This policy therefore aims to restore contestability by treating data scale, rather than data itself, as the source of market power and mandating interoperability and portability. Because the advantages compound over time, regulators must act decisively before lock-in becomes entrenched and dominance is established, while calibrating rules so they do not deter the investment that drives the gains. Data-sharing mandates may also introduce privacy and intellectual-property concerns, so contestability rules must be paired with clear governance for data access and merger review.

2. Workforce Transition and Reskilling Programmes

As companies continue to adopt AI-enabled automation systems to raise productivity, workers will continue to be displaced if their expertise is unable to meet the shifts in labour demand. Therefore, this policy manages displacement through transition rather than resisting it, with sector-specific reskilling linked to real roles in robotics maintenance, data analytics, and logistics coordination, turning displacement into movement towards higher-value work. Its limit is that reskilling alone cannot close the productivity–wage gap, because automation erodes workers’ bargaining power even where retraining succeeds, so it must be paired with wage insurance and labour protection and put in place before displacement accelerates.

3. Global Adjustment and Inclusive Supply Chains

As competitiveness shifts from cheap labour towards data and capital, developing economies that relied on labour-cost arbitrage face exclusion rather than mere adjustment, and left without access to the underlying capabilities that become a self-reinforcing gap. This lever uses an international policy approach to encourage technology transfer, development finance, and enabling trade rules to help these economies move up the value chain instead of being locked out. The structural obstacle is that advanced economies have weak incentives to assist future competitors. The answer is mutual benefit: as developing economies move into higher-value activities, advanced economies gain more resilient supplier networks and reduced geopolitical supply-chain risk, which is why co-funded infrastructure and supplier-upgrading partnerships can succeed.

Methods & Limitations

Forecast Approach

This forecast uses a scenario-based approach to examine how AI may reshape global logistics by 2030, drawing on two tiers of evidence. The first tier is peer-reviewed economic theory: the task model of automation (Acemoglu and Restrepo 2019), transaction-cost economics (Coase 1937; Williamson 1985), the economics of data (Jones and Tonetti 2020), the bullwhip effect (Lee, Padmanabhan and Whang 1997), and global value-chain research on cheap labour and production networks (Baglioni 2021). These sources provide the theoretical foundation for analysing productivity, labour displacement, data concentration, and changes in supply-chain geography. The second tier draws on industry, journalistic, and institutional sources, including McKinsey, the New York Times, the Reshoring Initiative, and the World Economic Forum, treated as indicative of direction and scale rather than precise measurement. Generative AI tools supported structuring, drafting, coding, and visual design, while the final argument, evidence selection, and policy judgement were reviewed and edited by the author.

Central Assumption

The central assumption is that AI adoption continues to expand from pilot projects into operational systems, particularly through predictive analytics, warehouse automation, route optimisation, and autonomous coordination tools. On this basis, the forecast is structured around three causal channels: firm-level productivity and concentration, labour-market displacement, and supply-chain geography.

Limitation

The pace and distribution of adoption remain uncertain, depending on data access, capital investment, workforce capability, regulation, and international coordination. The forecast is therefore limited by uncertainty and should be read as directional pathways rather than precise predictions.

Conclusion

By 2030, AI is likely to make global logistics more productive by reducing uncertainty, automating routine operations, and lowering coordination costs across supply networks. Without effective policy, productivity growth alone will not guarantee broad-based prosperity as the gains are more likely to concentrate among larger firms, higher-skilled workers, and advance economies, while smaller firms, routine workers, and developing economies bear more of the adjustment cost. The central question is therefore not whether AI can improve logistics, but whether institutions can shape its diffusion. Competition policy, workforce transition, and global adjustment will determine whether AI-driven logistics becomes a source of inclusive resilience or deeper economic concentration.
