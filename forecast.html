# AI and the Future of Global Logistics: Economic Transformation by 2030 # Thien Nhan Nguyen – S3972179 ## Landing / Theis statement Global logistics networks underpin modern trade, coordinating the movement of goods and services across increasingly complex and interconnected supply chains. Yet the industry continues to face persistent inefficiencies, forecasting errors, inventory misallocation, and transport delays that impose significant costs on firms and consumers through higher operating expenses, disrupted production, and inflationary pressure. This forecast argues that as agentic systems mature and adoption scales beyond pilots by 2030, AI will fundamentally reshape global logistics by reducing uncertainty and lowering coordination costs across supply networks but unevenly. Forecasting and optimisation can cut logistics costs by 5 to 20 percent (Oca et al. 2024), yet these gains compound with data scale, favouring hyperscale platforms and large logistics providers over the long tail of smaller suppliers. As a general-purpose technology, AI extends beyond physical automation into cognitive functions such as demand forecasting, route optimisation, and operational management that lower coordination costs across the chain. In doing so, it shifts competitive advantage away from labour-cost arbitrage and toward data-driven coordination and intelligence. While some efficiency gains are likely to be passed on to consumers through lower costs and improved services, the largest share will be captured by data-rich and more established firms while logistics workers in advance economies and developing ones bear the displacement. Consequently, the economic impact of AI in logistics will depend not only on technological capabilities but also on policy choices surrounding competition, workforce transition, and data governance.
